\section{Supplementary Information}\label{supplementary-information}

% Added in revision (R2.21): overview paragraph
This supplementary material provides methodological details and additional results for the main manuscript. The material is organised as follows. \textbf{Text A} describes the processing of person-movement tracking data, including the algorithm used to rejoin interrupted person tracks and the R Shiny application used to identify and manually link movements of TB patients. \textbf{Text B} describes the computation of air exchange rates from indoor CO\textsubscript{2} measurements using two complementary approaches: a transient mass balance model and a steady-state model. \textbf{Text C} presents the spatiotemporal Wells-Riley model used to estimate airborne \emph{Mtb} transmission risk, including the modelling assumptions, outcome measures, and the approach to estimate the effects of the interventions on spatial clustering and transmission. The \textbf{Supplementary results} section contains bioaerosol sampling results (Table~\ref{tab:S2}), a sensitivity analysis for spatial clustering measures using alternative kernel methods and metrics (Table~\ref{tab:S3}), and supplementary figures showing descriptive and modelled data (Figures~\ref{fig:S3}--\ref{fig:S11}).

\section{TEXT A: Processing of person movements}\label{text-a}

% Added in revision (R2.21): overview of the approach
\added{Optical sensors recorded anonymous person movements at 1-second intervals, producing time-stamped trajectories with position coordinates and unique person identifiers. However, raw tracking data contained interruptions---caused by temporary occlusion, sensor handoff, or movement outside the monitored area---that could fragment a single individual's visit into multiple short tracks. Furthermore, the tracking system did not identify which movements belonged to TB patients. Two post-processing steps were therefore required before the data could be used for transmission modelling: (1) automatically rejoining interrupted person tracks to better approximate continuous presence and exposure duration, and (2) manually identifying and linking the movements of TB patients using clinical records and an interactive R Shiny application. These steps are described in detail below.}

\subsection{Rejoining interrupted person movements}\label{rejoining-interrupted-person-movements}

We removed movements shorter than 10s to filter noise. Furthermore, people in the hospital could contribute multiple consecutive person tracks if they moved out of the range of a sensor (e.g. into a care room or other parts of the clinic) or were temporarily not recognised as a person (e.g. if their view was blocked by another person). To improve the continuity of tracks, we automatically rejoined interrupted movements using a matching algorithm similar to the one developed in our previous work \cite{banholzer2025PLOSComputBiol}. The algorithm proceeded forward in time and automatically linked two movements if they were close in time and proximity. The procedure was run iteratively in multiple rounds, with a stepwise increase of the time threshold and an alternation of the proximity threshold in each round. These thresholds were also location specific, i.e. if a movement ended in an area where people were typically seated, the time threshold was increased and the proximity threshold reduced, reflecting limited movement in these zones. In seated areas, the time threshold increased from 30s to 300s, and the proximity threshold alternated between 0.25m and 0.5m. In walking areas, the time threshold increased from 5s to 60s, and the proximity threshold alternated between 1m and 2m.

Automatic linkage increased the duration and completeness of person movements, thereby improving the estimation of \emph{Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb)} transmission risk. Without linkage, interruptions would result in missing exposure time. By linking interrupted movements, the model more accurately represented the true presence and exposure duration of each susceptible individual in the clinic.

\subsection{Identifying person movements of TB patients}\label{identifying-person-movements-of-tb-patients}

We used an R Shiny App to manually identify person movements most likely belonging to TB patients and to rejoin their interrupted movements (Figure \ref{fig:S1}). The setup to link interrupted movements was very similar to the one used in our previous study \cite{banholzer2025PLOSComputBiol}. Briefly, the right panel showed the floor plan with the currently selected movement in black and possible links in colours. The table below showed the time difference and distance to each link, as well as the difference in the last height, mean (sit) height and maximum (standing) height. In the left panel, the time and distance threshold could be varied and links could be made. It was possible to link movements forward or backward in time, and to revert previously made links. Every link and other change to the selected movement was automatically saved to a csv file. The descriptive information, floor plan, and table of possible links were updated after every action.

We used a fuzzy matching approach to identify movements of TB patients. Plausible candidates for TB patient movements could be moved between different containers. At first, all movements that were in the yellow TB registration area for more than 1 min were considered as ``possible'' movements of TB patients. Movements most likely belonging to TB patients could then be moved to the ``sure'' container. Such movements were typically located in the area marked in red on the floor plan for more than 2 min, where they sat on a chair while their clinical data was recorded. Furthermore, such movements often extended to the bottom-right part of the clinic, where people with presumptive TB provided a sputum sample (see ``sputum'' container). An additional container ``maybe'' was provided to consider movements just passing through the TB registration area, but the candidates from this container were only very rarely considered.

We (NB, RS, DK, LF) initially went through all movements in the ``possible'' container and moved the most plausible candidates for TB patients to the ``likely'' container. The ``likely'' candidates were assessed in more detail and interrupted movements rejoined. The most ``likely" candidates were then moved to the ``sure'' container to match in number the total number of clinical records shown at the top of the left panel. Interrupted movements of ``sure'' TB patient movements were rejoined as far as possible to improve continuous tracking and subsequent modelling of \emph{Mtb} infection risk. Without rejoining TB patient movements in particular, the total amount of infectious quanta generated by TB patients would otherwise be underestimated.

The line chart in the bottom of the right panel showed the timepoints for ``sure'' TB patient movements as they appeared in the area marked in red on the floor plan. The chart helped to verify that there were no implausible overlaps, since only one person at a time could sit on the chair in the red area and have their symptoms and patient characteristics recorded. Furthermore, the chart showed the order in which TB patient movements would ultimately be linked with the clinical records. The order was manually specified in the left panel. Since the exact timestamps in the clinical records were often incorrect, it was not possible to directly match clinical records with person movements based on the exact timestamps in the TB screening area. Instead, clinical records were sorted in ascending order and matched with person movements in the order in which they appeared in the red TB screening area.

We note that only the estimation of the absolute number of \emph{Mtb} infections in the clinic depended on accurate identification of TB patient tracks. In contrast, the effects of operational interventions on transmission were assessed in relative terms and therefore did not depend on the total person-time of TB patient movements (see Appendix C). Only a systematic misclassification of TB patient movements could meaningfully bias the relative effects. However, this is unlikely, as the dataset contained a large number of clear and uninterrupted TB patient movements, which served as reliable templates for identifying and linking additional, partially interrupted movements.

\section{TEXT B: Computation of air exchange rates}\label{text-b}

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{CO\textsubscript{2} levels in the hospital}{CO2 levels in the hospital}}\label{co2-levels-in-the-hospital}

We measured air quality in the waiting area of the clinic using two air quality monitoring devices (AQM1 and AQM2). AQM1 was placed near the area of the waiting hall with the highest spatial clustering of people, therefore its CO\textsubscript{2} levels were typically higher than those of AQM2 (Figure \ref{fig:S2}). We chose the CO\textsubscript{2} levels of AQM1 to compute air exchange rates (AERs), opting for a more conservative assessment of ventilation conditions in the clinic. AERs were computed using two different models, based on time-varying CO\textsubscript{2} levels and occupancy. For the main analysis, we used the AERs as computed by the transient mass balance model because it considers temporal variation in occupancy and CO\textsubscript{2} levels.

\includegraphics[width=5.90551in,height=3.69444in]{../../../../results/environmental_co2_over_time_by_device.png}
\caption{\textbf{Daytime CO\textsubscript{2} levels by device in the waiting area of the hospital.} AQM1 was placed where spatial clustering of people in the clinic was higher. Median as lines and interquartile range as ribbons.}
\label{fig:S2}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Transient mass balance model}\label{transient-mass-balance-model}

In the main analysis, we report the AER from the transient mass balance model. This model considers temporal variation in occupancy and CO\textsubscript{2} levels \cite{batterman2017IntJEnvResPublicHealth}. Formally,

\[CO_{t + 1} = \frac{6 \cdot 10^{4}\  \cdot n_{t} \cdot G}{Q} \cdot (1 - \exp( - QV\Delta t)) + (CO_{t} - CO_{out}) \cdot \exp( - QV\Delta t) + CO_{out}~,\]

where

\begin{itemize}
\item
\(CO_{t + 1}\) is the indoor CO\textsubscript{2} level (mixing ratio) at time \(t + 1\),
\item
\(n_{t}\) is the number of people in the clinic waiting area (occupancy),
\item
\(G\) is the average CO\textsubscript{2} generation rate per person (\(G =\)0.24L/min) \cite{persily2017IndoorAir},
\item
\(Q = AER/V\) is the outdoor air supply rate (estimated in m\textsuperscript{3}/s \(\cdot \frac{1}{3600})\),
\item
\(V\) is the volume of waiting hall (\(V\)=1179m\textsuperscript{3}),
\item
\(\Delta t\) is the time interval between measurements (\(\Delta t\)=5 min for air quality), and
\item
\(CO_{out}\) is the CO\textsubscript{2} mixing ratio in outdoor air (estimated but constrained to a range of \(CO_{out} = \lbrack 300,500\rbrack\) ppm).
\end{itemize}

We numerically solve the transient mass balance model with the limited memory Broyden--Fletcher--Goldfarb--Shanno algorithm (L-BFGS), a quasi-Newton method \cite{byrd1995SIAMJSciComput}. The optimal solution is the outdoor air supply rate \(Q\) that minimises the root mean square error. \(Q\) was then converted to the \(AER\) by multiplying it with the volume \(V\) of the clinic waiting hall.

\subsection{Steady state model}\label{steady-state-model}

Additionally, we estimated the AER using a steady state model. Under steady state conditions, CO\textsubscript{2} levels are assumed to reach a steady state mixing ratio \cite{batterman2017IntJEnvResPublicHealth}, in which case the AER can be computed as

\[AER\  = \ \frac{6 \cdot 10^{4}\  \cdot n_{\max} \cdot G}{V \cdot (CO_{\max} - CO_{out})}~,\]

where n\textsubscript{max} is the daily maximum number of people in the clinic's waiting area (peak occupancy) and CO\textsubscript{max} is the daily maximum indoor CO\textsubscript{2} level (peak concentration). \added{\(CO_{out}\) was taken to be the daily minimum indoor CO\textsubscript{2} level, which was reached during the night when the clinic was barely occupied.} 

\added{We compared the AERs between the steady state and transient mass balance model in Figure \ref{fig:S3}. The median AER from the transient mass balance model was 8\,h\textsuperscript{-1} (interquartile range [IQR] 6.2 to 10.8) compared to 6.2\,h\textsuperscript{-1} (IQR 5.7 to 7.6) for the steady state model. Conversely, the outdoor CO\textsubscript{2} level estimated by the transient mass balance model was 333\,ppm (IQR 302 to 348), which was lower than the minimum indoor  CO\textsubscript{2} level of 362\,ppm (IQR 353 to 372) used in the steady state model, although the two values were highly correlated (Pearson's $r$ 0.59, p$<$0.0001).}  

\section{TEXT C: Modelling airborne transmission risk and estimating the effects of interventions}\label{text-c}

\subsection{Spatiotemporal Wells-Riley model}\label{spatiotemporal-wells-riley-model}

In the Wells-Riley model \cite{riley1978AmJEpidemiol}, the risk of \emph{Mtb} infection (\(P\)) is estimated as the ratio of the number of diseased cases (\(D\)) among susceptible individuals (\(S\)) as

\[D/S = P = 1 - exp( - p \cdot N)~,\]

where \(p\) is the breathing rate and \(N\) is the cumulate quanta concentration (doses of infectious particles) that was generated by infectious individuals and to which susceptible individuals were exposed.

The Wells-Riley model assumes that the quanta concentration is the same in every location of the indoor space. In contrast, our spatiotemporal extension of the model allows for local variation of the quanta concentration in the indoor space over time \cite{banholzer2025PLOSComputBiol}. The area of the indoor space is thereby rasterised into a grid of cells \(c\) and each individual track of a person movement \(j\) is assigned to the nearest cell. The risk of infection is computed for each \(j\) as

\[P_{j} = 1 - \exp( - \sum_{c,t}^{}N_{c,t} \cdot f_{c}(j,t) \cdot p_{j}(a,t) \cdot 1s)~,\]

where \(f_{c}(j,t)\) is the indicator function (1 if \(j\) was in \(c\) at time \(t\), 0 otherwise) and \(p_{j}(a,t)\) is the breathing rate specific to activity \(a\). The spatiotemporal quanta concentration is estimated as

\[N_{c,t} = D\Delta{(N}_{c,t - 1} + \frac{1}{V_{c}} \cdot \sum_{i \in c}^{}{q_{i}(a,t) \cdot 1s)} \cdot \exp( - (AER_{t} + \lambda + k) \cdot 1s))~,\]

where \(N_{c,t}\)is the concentration in \(c\) at \(t\) (quanta/m\textsuperscript{3}), D is the diffusion constant (m/s\textsuperscript{2}), \(\Delta\) is the second-order Laplace operator, \(q_{i}(a,t)\) is the activity-specific quanta generation rate (quanta/s) by TB patient movement \(i\), \(V_{c}\) is the cell volume (m\textsuperscript{3}), \(AER_{t}\) is the air exchange rate, \(\lambda\) is the bacterial inactivation rate, and \(k\) is the gravitational settling rate (1/s).

\subsection{Modelling assumptions}\label{modelling-assumptions}

We made the same assumptions about the generation, diffusion, removal and inhalation of quanta as in our previous work \cite{banholzer2025PLOSComputBiol}. \added{The quanta generation rate was sampled from activity-specific lognormal prior distributions: median 1.08 quanta/h (95 quantile 0.003--386 quanta/h) while sitting and 2.81 quanta/h (95 quantile 0.008--987 quanta/h) while walking. The lognormal distribution has a long right tail, allowing for very high quanta generation rates in individual simulations and thus capturing the marked heterogeneity in infectiousness that is a key feature of TB epidemiology (see Figure E in the Supplementary Material of \cite{banholzer2025PLOSComputBiol} for the full distribution and individual estimates from the literature).}\label{chg:R2.23} The spatial diffusion constant was related to the air exchange rate, and the quanta removal rate was the sum of the air exchange, bacterial inactivation and gravitational settling rate. Since airflow and breathing direction were unknown, we assumed that the quanta diffused radially from the infectious source. We distinguished between walking and waiting activities based on whether a person covered a distance of more or less than 0.25m/s. We also assumed that the air was vertically well-mixed, meaning that the quanta concentration was the same within cubic cells. Although hospital staff consistently wore masks throughout the study as compared to hospital attendees, we did not model reduced quanta exposure due to masks to compare Mtb infection risk in isolation of other protective measures in place.

One deviation from our previous work was that we no longer simulated a random subset of asymptomatic TB patients as potential infectious quanta sources. Instead, we considered people with presumptive TB potentially infectious, as \emph{Mtb} has previously been detected in the exhaled breath of presumptive TB cases despite negative Sputum-Xpert test results \cite{patterson2024ProcNatlAcadSci}. This change reflects two methodological differences. First, the identification of presumptive TB cases was more systematic through routine symptom-based screening according to WHO guidelines, providing a clearer basis for defining potential infectious sources. Second, we could only manually rejoin interrupted person tracks of TB patients in this study, whereas in the previous study we could manually rejoin all tracks due to a shorter observation period (five days in the previous vs. 52 days in this study). Consequently, most random tracks of non-TB attendees would have been too short to meaningfully model quanta generation from asymptomatic individuals.

We rasterised the waiting area into a grid of cubic cells each with a squared area and a length of 0.5m. Person tracks were assigned to the nearest cell. Initial quanta generation was limited to the cell of the infectious source. To simplify modelling, we only considered the spread of quanta through the waiting area that was monitored by our optical sensors. We note that quanta may have spread further along the corridors into other parts of the clinic. In the rectangular grid of the waiting area, we masked cells pertaining to rooms or walls, which means that the quanta concentration was always zero in these cells.

We estimated the \emph{Mtb} infection risk under uncertainty using Monte Carlo simulations \cite{banholzer2025PLOSComputBiol,banholzer2024PLOSGlobalPublicHealth}. In each simulation, we assumed people with confirmed TB were always infectious and people with presumptive TB potentially infectious. The proportion of presumptive TB patients who were infectious was sampled from a Uniform(0.1, 0.3) prior, reflecting diagnostic test sensitivity of the Xpert MTB/RIF assay \cite{dorman2018LancetInfectDis}, and the patients were sampled at random. In the main analysis, people with confirmed and presumptive TB were considered equally infectious, but the quanta generation rates were sampled at random and thus could vary between TB patients in each model run.

Quanta generation and removal rates were sampled from lognormal prior distributions, thus the \emph{Mtb} infection risk varied considerably between simulations. We performed 100 Monte Carlo simulations and computed statistical quantities that were more robust to this variation (see below). We summarised our modelling results with the median and 95\%-quantile in the main text and show the full distribution in supplementary figures of Appendix D.

\subsection{Modelled outcome measures}\label{modelled-outcome-measures}

Our model returned the risk of infection \emph{P} per person movement \emph{j}. These risks were aggregated to compute the expected number of \emph{Mtb} infections \emph{EI} as

\[EI = \sum_{j}^{}P_{j}~.\]

This outcome was computed per day, study phase and overall. The hourly rate of infection \emph{R} was computed as

\[R = EI/PT \cdot 3600 \; \text{h}^{-1}~,\]

where \(PT = \sum_{j}^{}{}T_{j\ }\) is the total person-time in the clinic waiting area. The hourly risk of infection can be computed separately for female and male attendees and stratified by healthcare worker status. Finally, we computed the expected time until infection \emph{TTI} as

\[TTI = 1/R \; \text{h}~.\]

\subsection{Estimating the effects of interventions}\label{estimating-the-effects-of-interventions}

Our modelled risk of \emph{Mtb} transmission depended on proximity, but could also vary with the number of infectious people visiting the clinic. To isolate the effects of interventions targeting proximity (Table 1 in the main manuscript), we estimated the risk of TB infection using two model configurations:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  The first configuration assumed a non-uniform mixed (N\(UM\)) airspace, where the infection risk depended on the proximity to infectious individuals. In other words, the quanta concentration varied both over space and time as described above: \(N_{c,t}\).
\item
  The second configuration assumed a uniform mixed \((UM)\) airspace, where everyone in the waiting area at timepoint \(t\) had the same quanta exposure regardless of proximity to the infectious individual. In other words, the quanta concentration varied only over time and was equal to the average concentration in the space: \(N_{c,t} = {\bar{N}}_{t} = 1/\sum_{c}^{}{}N_{c,t}\).
\end{enumerate}

Accordingly, the transmission risks were denoted as \(P_{NUM}\) and \(P_{UM}\) and the expected number of infections as \({EI}_{NUM}\) and \({EI}_{UM}\). We computed the rate ratio as

\[RR = R_{NUM}/R_{UM} = EI_{NUM}/EI_{UM}~.\]

Since the total person-time is identical for both model configurations, it cancels out. Note that as spatial clustering of people decreases, the physical distance between individuals increases (proximity decreases), which reduces the risk of short-range, high particle-density exposure. Thus, we would expect that interventions targeting proximity reduce the rate ratio.

We modelled the effects of interventions on the daily rate ratio as

\[\log(RR_{d}) - \ log(PT_{d}) = \alpha\  \cdot Weekday_{d} + \beta \cdot Phase_{d}~,\]

where \(\log(RR_{d})\) is the log rate ratio on day \(d\), \(log(PT_{d})\) is the log of the total person time, \(Weekday_{d}\ \)are binary indicators for the day of the week (Monday to Sunday, with Monday as the reference), and \(Phase_{d}\) are binary indicators for the study phase (first and second intervention, with baseline as reference). We adjusted our estimates for weekday fixed-effects because our study team noted difficulties in enforcing interventions on some days, especially Mondays, when the clinic was most frequented and patients tended to be less compliant with interventions.

The rationale for \(\log(PT_{d})\) as a model offset is the following. Higher occupancy naturally increases the number of close-proximity interactions and thus the rate ratio. Therefore, we used \(\log(PT_{d})\) as a model offset, so that the effects of interventions on \emph{Mtb} transmission only reflect the changes in the spatial clustering of people in the clinic waiting area. In the main text, we refer to \(RR_{d}/PT_{d}\) as the adjusted rate ratio. Note that while the daily expected number of \emph{Mtb} infections showed a decreasing trend over the study (Figure \ref{fig:S9}) -- probably reflecting reduced clinic attendance of both infectious and susceptible patients -- the adjusted rate ratio was more stable (Figure \ref{fig:S10}).

Finally, we estimated how many \emph{Mtb} infections may have been prevented by interventions. For this, we obtained posterior draws for the daily log rate \({\widetilde{RR}}_{d}\) ratios from our Bayesian model assuming routine conditions throughout the study. These log rate ratios were converted to the number of \emph{Mtb} infections without interventions according to the NUM model \({\widetilde{EI}}_{NUM}\), assuming the same number of infections according to the UM model \({EI}_{UM}\) and total person time \(PT_{d}\). We then computed the number of prevented infections as the difference between \({\widetilde{EI}}_{NUM}\) (without interventions) and \({EI}_{NUM}\) (with interventions).
